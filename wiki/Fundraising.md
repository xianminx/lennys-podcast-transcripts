# Fundraising

How to raise money, when to raise money, and whether to raise money at all -- synthesized from 35+ Lenny's Podcast episodes with founders, VCs, and operators who have been on both sides of the table.

---

## Should You Raise at All?

The most important fundraising question isn't "how" -- it's "whether." Several guests argue persuasively that the default assumption (startup = raise VC) is wrong.

### The Case Against Raising

> "Silicon Valley has found a way to make the most profitable style of business the least profitable." -- Jason Fried

Jason Fried ran 37signals/Basecamp for 24 years -- profitable, no investors, no board, 100K+ customers, double-digit million dollar annual profits. His argument: software margins should be 80-90%, but VC-backed companies barely break even because the money erases scrappiness and comes with "very thick and strong strings attached" that prevent a full range of motion.

> "There is really only one outcome for venture-backed companies, which is go huge." -- Jason Fried

Andrew Wilkinson (Tiny, ~$300M revenue across 75+ businesses) puts it similarly:

> "The only difference between what we do and what a venture capitalist does is the level of tolerance of burning money on fire." -- Andrew Wilkinson

Edwin Chen built Surge AI to $1B+ without playing the Silicon Valley game:

> "I wish I'd known you could build a company by being heads down... You don't need to spend all your time fundraising." -- Edwin Chen

Matt MacInnis (Rippling CPO) goes further, warning that the never-quit mantra serves VCs more than founders:

> "The Silicon Valley try-until-you-die mindset is not pro-entrepreneur, it's pro-venture capitalist." -- Matt MacInnis

### When Raising Makes Sense

Geoffrey Moore offers two clear criteria:

1. **The technology is too expensive to self-fund** (hardware, biotech, deep infrastructure)
2. **The market is catching fire and speed matters** -- competitors will outrun you without capital

> "Just because you want to do a startup doesn't mean you need venture capital." -- Geoffrey Moore

Patrick Campbell (bootstrapped ProfitWell to a $200M+ exit) offers a litmus test:

> "If you don't think there's a clear path to a billion in revenue per year, it's probably not venture-scale." -- Patrick Campbell

Interestingly, Campbell admitted he wished he'd raised VC in hindsight -- sometimes the market moves faster than a bootstrap can fund.

### The Middle Path

Dan Shipper (Every) invented the **"sip seed round"**: $2M from Reid Hoffman on a SAFE with a pull-down-whenever-you-want structure. If the company raised more, it converts to equity; if not, investors could convert in 3 years. This preserved optionality between venture-scale and lifestyle business.

## How Investors Decide

### At Early Stage: It's About You

> "Early-stage investors invest because: 'I like the CEO. I like the story. That's it.'" -- Uri Levine (Waze)

Uri Levine (co-founder of Waze) says investors decide whether they like you **before they sit down**. First impressions are everything. He recommends the CEO go to meetings alone -- no distractions, full spotlight on you.

Gustaf Alstromer (Y Combinator) notes that excellent communication skills are "correlated with enjoying doing fundraising," while technical co-founders "don't really care that much about fundraising."

Jessica Livingston (YC co-founder) emphasizes:

> "Confidence is very important, especially when it comes to fundraising." -- Jessica Livingston

### At Later Stages: Proof Points Matter

Todd Jackson (First Round Capital) maps fundraising stages to [[Product-Market Fit]] levels:

| PMF Level | Stage | What Investors Want |
|-----------|-------|---------------------|
| Level 1 | Pre-seed / Seed | Compelling founder + insight |
| Level 2 | Seed / Series A | Early retention + engagement signals |
| Level 3 | Series B | Scalable unit economics |
| Level 4 | Series C/D+ | Clear path to category dominance |

Annie Duke (First Round) revealed their internal test: "Have you ever had a company that exited for more than a billion dollars that did not fund at Series A? The answer is no."

### The VC Perspective

Sahil Mansuri warns that VCs are fundamentally salespeople:

> "VCs are salespeople. 100% of VC is a salesperson." -- Sahil Mansuri

His advice: **give the VC every possible out**. If they still want to talk after you've highlighted every risk, they're for real.

Ben Horowitz (a16z) on the AI funding market:

> "The great company and the crap company both get funded. But that's just venture capital. That's not a bubble." -- Ben Horowitz

## The Art of the Pitch

### The 3-Slide Framework (Mike Maples Jr., Floodgate)

Mike Maples Jr. -- who has the original pitch decks for Airbnb, Pinterest, and other legendary companies -- boils the pitch down to three essential slides:

1. **Slide 1: What you do, as if I know nothing.** "We're Airbnb, we let you rent an extra room in your house." NOT "A marketplace for unused residential housing space."
2. **Slide 2: Here's the thing we know that's not obvious.** Your unique insight about the world.
3. **Slide 3: What proof points do you have?**

Critical warning -- avoid the **"Franken deck"**:

> Taking advice from 20 people who don't believe in your vision creates a bloated deck that obscures your message. -- Mike Maples Jr.

His meta-advice to founders:

> "It's okay if most investors don't like your idea. What you want to find is the ones who are prepared to move with you." -- Mike Maples Jr.

### Start with Your Strongest Point (Uri Levine)

> "The most important slide is the title/cover slide -- it's displayed the longest. Put your strongest point there." -- Uri Levine

Uri recommends starting AND ending with your strongest point. Good stories need **details** to be authentic -- brief use cases don't resonate. Create **emotional engagement**, not just facts.

### Start with the Insight, Not the Market (Merci Grace)

> "Every mediocre pitch deck starts with 'here's the market.' Start with what makes YOU unique." -- Merci Grace

Merci Grace got her first term sheet before she even knew what VC was. Her advice: lead with your unique insight.

### Strategic Narrative (Andy Raskin)

Andy Raskin transformed fundraising outcomes by restructuring pitches around storytelling principles:

> "We didn't change the product... just how we talked about it." -- Andy Raskin

After restructuring around a strategic narrative, the company got a term sheet months later.

### The Presentation Paradox

Mike Maples Jr. notes that Airbnb's original pitch was a "complete catastrophe presentation" -- but it didn't matter because the insight was powerful. Bad presentations don't mean bad investments.

## Surviving Rejection

Rejection is the defining experience of fundraising. The data:

- **Uri Levine**: Fundraising is a 1% game. VCs see 100-200 companies per year, invest in 1-2. Expect "the dance of 100 nos."
- **Melanie Perkins (Canva)**: Pitched 100+ investors and was rejected by every single one before finding backers.
- **Grant Lee (Gamma)**: First investor called it "the worst pitch, worst idea I have ever heard" and hung up. Now at $100M+ ARR, $2B+ valuation.
- **Sriram Krishnan**: "Emailed 250 founders, took 85 meetings, got 30 checks. Seed round took eight months." Was told "nobody will invest because your accent is too strong."
- **Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot)**: "We could not raise capital. The common argument was there literally is exactly one company in the history of software that's made a global brand, billions of dollars, in SMB."
- **Keith Yandell (DoorDash)**: Series D was a near-death experience -- "very little runway left, we almost went out of business."

### How to Use Rejection

Melanie Perkins turned each "no" into iteration fuel:

> "I thought the investors were wrong, frankly." -- Melanie Perkins

Her method: when investors said "your market's not big enough," she added a market size slide. When they said "you're the same as X," she added a competitive landscape slide. Each rejection made the deck stronger.

> "Focus on what you can control: your pitch deck and the number of people you talk to." -- Melanie Perkins

Keith Yandell distills it to:

> "It only takes one yes. But you got to keep going." -- Keith Yandell (DoorDash)

## Capital Strategy & Timing

### Always Be Funded

> "Always be funded is good advice." -- Uri Levine

Uri Levine's rule: keep **18 months of runway** (12 months if you're a bigger risk taker). Cash is the best crisis preparation. When Waze's term sheet fell through after Google announced turn-by-turn navigation, their runway gave them time to recover.

### Each Round Should Change Your Value State

Geoffrey Moore frames each fundraise in terms of **risk de-layering**:

| Round | Risk Removed | Milestone |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Angel / Pre-seed | "Can you create anything?" | Working prototype |
| Seed / Series A | "Will anyone use this?" | Crossed the chasm -- single use case dominance |
| Series B | "Can this be a real business?" | Scalable unit economics |
| Growth (C/D+) | "How big can this get?" | Scaling from $10M to $100M+ |

> "With a single round of funding, you should be able to cross the chasm and dominate a single use case in a single market within 18 to 24 months." -- Geoffrey Moore

His growth pattern: **triple-double / double-triple** -- $1M → $4M → $12M → $24M → $48M → $96M.

### Conservative Valuations as Strategy

DoorDash "put forth conservative numbers, not stretch plans, which cost dilution upfront but built deep trust with investors." -- Keith Yandell

Retool was "very conservative in how they raised money and the valuations they raised at. Which, looking back, ended up being a really good idea." -- Eeke de Milliano

Cam Adams (Canva) shares a cautionary tale: a lead investor tried to **cut valuation by 50%** two days before signing. The founders flew to Silicon Valley and found a new lead in a week with better terms. The experience drove Canva to focus on profitability:

> "We never want to be put in the situation where we have to go to someone for money." -- Cam Adams

### Using FOMO

Uri Levine on leveraging competitive dynamics:

> "May I remind you that you did not invest in Twitter at $55 million valuation." -- Uri Levine (to Qualcomm Ventures)

## Tactical Playbook

### Before You Talk to Investors

Dalton Caldwell (YC) warns about the most common mistake:

> "Start doing customer validation first versus building a PowerPoint deck, versus trying to raise money." -- Dalton Caldwell

Many companies say "We're focused on raising our pre-seed round before we talk to customers." That's backwards.

### Working Backwards Framework (Jonathan Lowenhar)

Companies are always working backwards from one of four things:
1. **Exit**
2. **Next fundraise**
3. **Profitability**
4. **Winding down**

Most founders are working backwards from the next fundraise. You must codify exactly **what has to be true** to unlock it.

### The Internal Story = The External Story

> "A lot of the story you tell to investors, early ones especially, is the story you should be telling internally to anybody you hire... don't just use it for fundraising." -- Claire Hughes Johnson (ex-Stripe COO)

### Getting PR for Your Round

Emilie Gerber (Six Eastern) on funding announcements:

- TechCrunch covers ~9 funding stories/week out of ~50 actual rounds
- **Be very direct**: "Here's the amount raised, here's the space, here are the investors"
- Don't do a straight funding announcement -- use it as a news hook for a larger story
- Offer exclusives to top outlets
- Business Insider runs "pitch deck" stories -- submit yours

### Drew Houston's Seed Round Advice

> "How do you raise a seed round? Talk to someone who did it a year ago, not the CEO who says 'I don't remember.'" -- Drew Houston (Dropbox)

Houston's seed was facilitated by Pejman Nozad, an angel investor who ran a rug store. "That was on a Friday, and then Saturday Mike Moritz is in our apartment." Dropbox went from $6M to $4B valuation in 4 years.
