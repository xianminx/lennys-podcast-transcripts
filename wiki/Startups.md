# Startups

Frameworks, mental models, and hard-won lessons on building startups from zero -- from founding through scaling -- synthesized from 20+ Lenny's Podcast episodes with founders, VCs, and operators.

---

## Finding and Validating Ideas

### Fall in Love with the Problem, Not the Solution

Uri Levine (co-founder of Waze) built two billion-dollar companies around one principle:

> "The entrepreneurship journey is about value creation. The simplest way to create value is solve a problem." -- Uri Levine

His litmus test for how you pitch reveals what you actually care about:

> "If you start your story with 'Our company is' or 'Our system is,' you focus on your solution. If you start with 'The problem we are solving is,' then you focus on the problem. If your story starts with 'The value that we create for you is,' then you focus on the user. The last two are way better." -- Uri Levine

To validate the problem, talk to 100 people:

> "You don't need a hundred, but the hundred basically says you need to get out of your comfort zone." -- Uri Levine

And you must genuinely care:

> "You need to fall in love. You personally need to fall in love with the problem... there is not enough internal drive to take you through the hardship of the journey." -- Uri Levine

### Pattern Breakers: Inflections, Insights, and Founder-Future Fit

Mike Maples Jr. (Floodgate) identifies three elements of breakthrough startup ideas:

1. **Inflection**: An external event that creates the potential for radical change. Types: technological, regulatory, belief changes.
2. **Insight**: A non-obvious truth about how one or more inflections can be harnessed to change behavior. Must be non-consensus AND right.
3. **Founder-Future Fit**: The founder is uniquely suited to see and execute on this future.

> "Better doesn't matter when you're a startup... A startup wins by avoiding the comparison trap entirely." -- Mike Maples Jr.

His key mental model: great founders **backcast**, not forecast:

> "Great founders say, it's a given that the future has to be radically different for me to be a big winner, and so I'm going to look for radically different futures and work backwards." -- Mike Maples Jr.

### Column B Thinking

Melanie Perkins (Canva) distinguishes two modes of thinking:

- **Column A** = looking at the bricks around you and stacking them (incremental)
- **Column B** = dreaming of the perfect vision and working backward (transformative)

> "Imagine what is the future that you actually want? What is the world that you want to live in?" -- Melanie Perkins

> "You kind of want to have a ladder that goes all the way up to the moon, which is your crazy, wild vision. And then you want to have rungs that just work its way up step by step." -- Melanie Perkins

### The #1 Mistake: Not Talking to Customers

Gustaf Alstromer (Y Combinator) says this is the most common startup killer:

> "Startups fail because they don't talk to customers. And if you don't talk to customers or users, you don't actually know what's important." -- Gustaf Alstromer

Practical guidance:
- **Talk to 25-50 potential customers** (reach out to many more, since only ~10% are early adopters)
- **Watch, don't just ask**: "Have them screen share, have them walk you through their daily workflow"
- **Beware false positives**: "People confuse external validation with the thing that matters the most"

## Going from 0 to 1

### The Four Phases of a Startup

Uri Levine maps the startup journey into sequential phases:

| Phase | Focus | Key Rule |
|-------|-------|----------|
| 1 | Find [[Product-Market Fit]] | Product only -- no sales/marketing needed |
| 2 | Figure out growth OR business model | Order depends on frequency of use |
| 3 | Figure out the other | Complete the business equation |
| 4 | Scale everything | Now hire, expand, invest |

> "Focus is not about what we are doing, it's about what we are not doing. These are the hard decisions." -- Uri Levine

For high-frequency products: growth comes organically through word of mouth, so figure out the business model second. For low-frequency products: prove the business model first.

### Founder-Led Sales

Jen Abel (JJELLYFISH) on the founder's role in early sales:

> "The founder is the product. When people say, 'What does that mean?' It means that you are a subject matter expert on something highly specific." -- Jen Abel

Her tactical advice:
- Start with **30 manual outreach attempts** before using tools
- Lead cold emails with something unexpected (e.g., "Zero to one sales talent doesn't exist. That's why I want to have a conversation with you.")
- The founder sells credibility and vision; a hired salesperson can't replicate this early

### Getting Your First Users

**Consumer apps** -- Nikita Bier built 15 apps before finding PMF. His sequential validation:

1. Will people use the core flow?
2. Will people spread it within their peer group?
3. Will it hop peer groups?

> "For the first 100 users, yes, that's how we got them -- manual recruitment. And that allowed us to know whether the product was working or not." -- Nikita Bier

**B2B SaaS** -- Raaz Herzberg (Wiz) did 10-15 customer calls PER DAY in the first weeks. The key signal:

> "Customers pushing for next steps -- 'Wait, how are you pricing this? When can we start a POV?' -- vs. 'Oh, this sounds interesting.'" -- Raaz Herzberg

She warns about the wrong way to listen:

> "We were looking for positive reinforcements, but not really listening intently to signs of deep enthusiasm." -- Raaz Herzberg

**The "Do things that don't scale" benchmark** -- Todd Jackson cites Vanta's Christina Cacioppo, who manually filled out SOC 2 spreadsheets before building any product. At Level 1, it's OK to be inefficient.

## Co-Founder Dynamics

> "65% of startups fail because of co-founder conflict." -- Rachel Lockett

### Key Practices

1. **Self-awareness first**: Use tools like the Enneagram. "What do I bring into this dynamic, and how am I being experienced by the other person?"
2. **Co-founder vows**: Consciously commit to decision-making norms and conflict resolution before a crisis hits
3. **Regular check-ins**: Weekly virtual, quarterly in-person. "Just like couples need a date night, co-founders need time and space to connect."
4. **Name the classic tension**: CEO = visionary optimist vs. CTO = skeptical realist. "This creates an inherent tension between blind optimism and skepticism." Naming it defuses it.

### Designing Your Role

Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot CTO) made a rule from Day 1: zero direct reports, ever.

> "If I can get all the upside of scale without the downsides of scale... that's what keeps me engaged and energized." -- Dharmesh Shah

His co-founder Brian Halligan became CEO. The lesson: founders can -- and should -- design roles that match their strengths rather than defaulting to conventional titles.

## When to Pivot vs. Persist

### Stewart Butterfield's Cold Rationality Test

Butterfield pivoted twice -- from a game to Flickr, and from another game to Slack. His framework:

> "The default advice for anyone in anything is persevere... I think you have to really be coldly rational." -- Stewart Butterfield

> "It's fucking humiliating. I convinced so many people to get a company off the ground... for a lot of people, it feels better to just keep doing it until it dies of suffocation." -- Stewart Butterfield

His test: **Have you exhausted every reasonable idea?** "I think by that point I had exhausted every non-ridiculously long shot idea to make it commercially successful."

### Uri Levine's Pivot Algorithm

1. "If I would start today, is this what I'm going to do?" If yes, keep going.
2. Do you have tech, team, or know-how that gives you an advantage?
3. Do you have the passion and energy?
4. Does your team want to go after this?
5. Can you get funding?

### The Courage to Pivot Hard

Varun Mohan (Windsurf) pivoted overnight from a profitable GPU infrastructure business:

> "You should never be too in love with your ideas... If you have an organization that is very truth-seeking... morale is not going to be low if you made the wrong decision." -- Varun Mohan

Scott Wu (Cognition/Devin) pivoted eight times within coding agents in 18 months.

Todd Jackson quotes Lattice's Jack Altman:

> "Most founders do a 10% pivot, and what they need to be doing is a 200% pivot." -- Jack Altman

Dalton Caldwell (YC) adds:

> "A successful pivot gets warmer instead of colder from what you're an expert at." -- Dalton Caldwell

### Tarpit Ideas

Dalton Caldwell warns about ideas that trap founders:

> "By definition it is only a tarpit if it seems like it's not. The weird aspect is an idea that a lot of people come up with and then it seems like an unsolved problem and you get lots of positive feedback." -- Dalton Caldwell

Classic tarpit: an app to coordinate with friends to decide where to go out at night. Lots of positive feedback, zero traction.

## Speed and Execution

### The Dehydrated Company

Varun Mohan (Windsurf) runs a deliberately lean operation:

> "I want the company to almost be this dehydrated entity and every hire is like a little bit of water. And we only go back and hire someone when we're back to being dehydrated." -- Varun Mohan

> "You don't win by doing 10 things well. You win by doing one thing really well and maybe you fail nine things... In school you optimize for your total GPA. But for companies, I just need to get an A+ on the one class that matters." -- Varun Mohan

### Speed as Culture

Scott Wu (Cognition/Devin) on pace:

> "We had a hackathon in November, another in December, started the company officially in January, got prototypes out in February, did a launch in March, got our first customers in April... basically truly pushing the pace in every spot." -- Scott Wu

Uri Levine on failing fast:

> "If you fail fast, you still have plenty of time to try another attempt... the more attempts that you have, you simply increase the likelihood of being successful." -- Uri Levine

> "The biggest enemy of good enough is perfect. You need to be good enough in order to win the market." -- Uri Levine

### Creating Velocity as a Signal

Tanguy Crusson (Atlassian) on building momentum:

> "Give people a sense of velocity and speed. No one wants to fuck with a high-speed train." -- Tanguy Crusson

Tactic: weekly demos to show progress. Save finished features for weeks when nothing else ships to maintain the illusion of continuous velocity.

## Scaling: From Startup to Growth

### Giving Away Your Legos

Molly Graham (Facebook, CZI) on the hardest scaling challenge:

> "Scaling is about giving away your Legos." -- Molly Graham

> "Your job is to build a company that would make a decision the way the founder would when they're not in the room." -- Molly Graham

### The Code Rewrite Tunnel

Melanie Perkins (Canva) went through a two-year code rewrite that blocked all product shipping:

> "A product company not shipping product is not really a recipe for fun." -- Melanie Perkins

They used a rubber ducky game board to gamify the tunnel and keep morale alive.

### Deliberate Understaffing

Matt MacInnis (Rippling CPO) argues for staying lean:

> "When you hire for a role and you already have enough people there, you get a lot of weird politics." -- Varun Mohan

The Silicon Valley instinct to hire ahead of need often creates more problems than it solves.

## Founder Mindset and Psychology

### The Emotional Reality

> "I slept like a baby. I woke up every two hours and cried." -- Ben Horowitz (quoted by Uri Levine)

> "Building a startup is a journey from one crisis to the next." -- Uri Levine

> "Starting a company is incredibly lonely... founders are competitive. They don't want to look bad." -- Gustaf Alstromer

### Irrational Optimism + Brutal Realism

> "You need to be irrationally optimistic... But then you also need to be really, really realistic." -- Varun Mohan

> "I wish I had... this idea of just being okay with being wrong faster." -- Varun Mohan

### Not Everyone Should Be a Founder

Tom Conrad offers a contrarian but important perspective:

> "There's this belief that everybody needs to be a founder. I think, in some ways, our industry would be much better off if there were fewer founders." -- Tom Conrad

> "You can achieve your goals in collaboration with others. You don't have to be the person that raises the seed round." -- Tom Conrad

### Conviction Through Rejection

> "I thought the investors were wrong, frankly." -- Melanie Perkins (after 100+ rejections)

> "Never give up is the most important behavior of a successful CEO of a startup." -- Uri Levine

> "Making decisions with conviction... If you don't make them with conviction, then the team is not going to follow." -- Uri Levine

## Building Culture Early

### The 30-Day Firing Rule

Uri Levine's most actionable culture advice:

> "Mark your calendars for 30 days down the road and ask yourself one question: 'Knowing what I know today, would I hire this person?'" -- Uri Levine

If yes, tell them and give them more equity. If no, fire immediately.

> "In a small place like a startup... everyone knows when someone shouldn't be there and the CEO doesn't do anything." -- Uri Levine

### Culture as Values in Action

Melanie Perkins made "Crazy Big Goals" a Canva value, coupled with celebrations (smashing plates, releasing doves, La Tomatina festivals):

> "If you go and try and take some bricks from someone else's house and stick it in your house, it's probably going to not look very matched." -- Melanie Perkins

Don't import another company's culture. Build your own.

### Generosity as Strategy

Stewart Butterfield (Slack) built culture through generosity: 100% health insurance, fair billing (stopped charging for unused seats), extended severance with help finding new jobs.

> "In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers." -- Stewart Butterfield

He made everyone chant this at all-hands meetings.

### Design the Company for the Founders

Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot):

> "It's amazing what you can get away with in terms of shaping the universe to your liking." -- Dharmesh Shah

No meetings before 11am. Zero reports for the CTO. The company should serve the founders' working styles, not the other way around.
