# Product-Market Fit

The single most important milestone in a startup's life -- what it is, how to measure it, how to find it, and what to do after. Synthesized from 17+ Lenny's Podcast episodes with the people who defined and operationalized PMF.

---

## What Is Product-Market Fit?

There is no single definition, but every description converges on the same feeling: **pull**.

> "Product market fit is really simple. That means that you create value to your customers. If you do not figure out product market fit, you will die. As simple as that. You never heard of a company that did not figure out product market fit. They simply died." -- Uri Levine (Waze)

> "Nobody really knows what product market fit is. Everybody has a different explanation, but it means people want to use your product. That they're clamoring for it." -- Tamar Yehoshua

> "The fish are jumping into the boat. The rock is rolling down the hill and I'm trying to chase it instead of pushing it up the hill." -- Todd Jackson (First Round Capital)

The most vivid metaphor comes from Figma's early days. Dylan Field was demoing Figma to a potential customer who literally **pulled the laptop out of his hands** to use it:

> "That story of the potential customer pulling the laptop from Dylan is such a good metaphor for product market fit, which people describe as you feel pull. Someone's literally pulling it from your hands." -- Claire Butler (Figma)

### The Desperation Threshold

Mike Maples Jr. (Floodgate) frames it even more starkly:

> "Customer has to be so desperate that when they see what you have, they're like, 'I've got to have it.'" -- Mike Maples Jr.

> "The only chance for a startup to win is to win on a playing field where better doesn't matter, where execution isn't as important as 'Got to have that thing. I'll take any version of it you give me because I am desperate for it.'" -- Mike Maples Jr.

## Measuring PMF

### The Sean Ellis Test (40% "Very Disappointed")

The most widely used PMF survey, created by Sean Ellis:

> "The question is, how would you feel if you could no longer use this product? Very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, not disappointed." -- Sean Ellis

> "If 40% or more of people say they'd be very disappointed if they can no longer use the product, you essentially have product market fit." -- Sean Ellis

**When to run it:**
- Survey users who have **really used** the product (not just signed up)
- Minimum: used the product 2+ times
- Ideally used it within the last 1-2 weeks
- Minimum sample size: ~30 responses

**Follow-up questions:**
1. "What is the primary benefit that you get?" (open-ended)
2. "Why is that benefit important to you?" (gets to emotional context)
3. "What would you use instead if this product were no longer available?" (competitive landscape)

Ellis himself clarifies this is a leading indicator:

> "I would say it's a leading indicator of product market fit. The lightning indicator is, do they actually keep using it? So probably retention cohorts are more accurate." -- Sean Ellis

### Cultural Calibration

Jag Duggal (Nubank) learned that the 40% threshold needs cultural adjustment:

> "Brazilians are inherently polite and more optimistic than average." -- Jag Duggal

Nubank uses a **50% threshold** instead of 40%.

### Retention as the One True Metric

Uri Levine cuts through the complexity:

> "Product market fit have one metric. One metric. That's it. Retention." -- Uri Levine

> "If they're not coming back, that means that you are not creating value." -- Uri Levine

### NPS as a Word-of-Mouth Proxy

Nilan Peiris (Wise) discovered an exponential relationship between NPS and referrals:

- NPS 6 → 7-8: referrals doubled
- NPS 8 → 9: doubled again
- NPS 9 → 10: doubled again

> "If you move the NPS from 30% to 50%, you increase the viral coefficient of your customer base." -- Nilan Peiris

Wise achieved NPS of 70% -- higher than iPhone and Google Search. But Sarah Tavel (Benchmark) prefers Sean Ellis over NPS:

> "I don't like NPS for this. I like Sean Ellis's question of how disappointed would you be if this product disappeared?" -- Sarah Tavel

### Organic Growth as Signal

Multiple guests converge on organic/word-of-mouth growth as the strongest PMF indicator:

> "When you're early on, your mindset should almost be like you're trying to create a word of mouth machine. If you can get that part right, everything else becomes significantly easier." -- Grant Lee (Gamma)

> "In order to get hypergrowth, you have to have organic, inbound, and viral word of mouth. You can't pay enough to grow at those rates and have a viable company." -- Carilu Dietrich

## The PMF Engine (Rahul Vohra, Superhuman)

Rahul Vohra turned the Sean Ellis survey into a systematic engine for increasing PMF:

> "You can measure product market fit. You can optimize product market fit. You can systematically, even numerically increase product market fit. And you can even have an algorithm write your roadmap for you." -- Rahul Vohra

### The Algorithm

1. **Segment** your users by who says "very disappointed"
2. **Identify** the primary benefit that "very disappointed" users love
3. **Filter** "somewhat disappointed" users to those who value the same benefit
4. **Ask** the "somewhat disappointed" group what they don't like
5. **Build** that list: half your time doubling down on what people love, half systematically overcoming objections

> "This question, this metric is way more predictive of success than something, for example, like net promoter score." -- Rahul Vohra

### Operationalizing at Scale

Jag Duggal (Nubank) took this further:

> "We rarely scale a project, a product we've launched, until we know the Sean Ellis score and we know that it's hit a threshold that we find really compelling." -- Jag Duggal

They use it at the **feature level**, not just the product level. Post-launch they layer on NPS and churn metrics.

## The Four Levels of PMF (Todd Jackson, First Round Capital)

The most comprehensive framework for understanding PMF as a spectrum:

> "Finding product-market fit is the single most important thing that your startup does in the first three years, and it's just underexplored and underexplained as a topic." -- Todd Jackson

| Level | Name | Customers | ARR | Focus | Duration |
|-------|------|-----------|-----|-------|----------|
| L1 | Nascent | 3-5 | <$500K | Satisfaction | 12-18 months |
| L2 | Developing | 5-25 | $500K-$5M | Demand | 12-18 months |
| L3 | Strong | 25-100+ | $5M-$25M+ | Efficiency | Varies |
| L4 | Extreme | 100+ | $25M+ | All three | 4-6 years total |

### Three Dimensions

Todd Jackson defines extreme PMF as:

> "A state of widespread demand for a product that satisfies a critical need and crucially can be delivered repeatably and efficiently to each customer." -- Todd Jackson

The three dimensions: **demand, satisfaction, efficiency**. You must achieve all three.

### Where Companies Get Stuck

> "The majority of startups do not get past level one or level two... probably 60 to 70% are going to get stuck." -- Todd Jackson

### The Danger of False PMF

WeWork and Casper achieved satisfaction and demand but never efficiency:

> "Those products managed to achieve customer satisfaction and demand, but they never got the efficiency right, and so the whole business just never worked at scale." -- Todd Jackson

### The Four Ps for Pivoting

When stuck, change one or more of these: **Persona, Problem, Promise, Product.**

> "Most founders do a 10% pivot, and what they need to be doing is a 200% pivot." -- Jack Altman (Lattice), via Todd Jackson

## How Long Does It Take?

The answer is consistently: **years**.

> "For Waze, it was four years. For Microsoft, it was five years. For Netflix, it was 10 years... ChatGPT... they're seven years old. It took them six years until you heard about them." -- Uri Levine

> "For the best enterprise companies, I would say they tend to reach extreme product-market fit in roughly four to six years." -- Todd Jackson

Even at massive scale, uncertainty persists:

> "Even at 100 million, he wasn't sure they had product-market fit." -- Todd Jackson, about Ali Ghodsi (Databricks)

## Signs You DON'T Have PMF

### The Push vs. Pull Test

If every customer feels like the last you'll ever find, you don't have it:

> "Every customer we got... felt like the last customer we were ever going to find. It felt like rolling the stone uphill, and if you stop pushing, it's going to roll back on you and crush you." -- David Hsu (Retool), on the pre-PMF experience

### Vanity Metrics Trap

Grant Lee (Gamma) won Product Hunt's product of the day, week, AND month -- but knew they didn't have PMF because organic growth wasn't taking off:

> "If we just kind of played things out, we knew that the product wasn't going to grow on its own. Something was missing." -- Grant Lee

### Tarpit Ideas

Dalton Caldwell (YC) warns about ideas that generate positive feedback but zero traction:

> "By definition it is only a tarpit if it seems like it's not. An idea that a lot of people come up with, seems like an unsolved problem, and you get lots of positive feedback." -- Dalton Caldwell

### Less Than 40% on Sean Ellis

If you're below the threshold, Sean Ellis warns against a common mistake:

> "If you start paying attention to what your somewhat disappointed users are telling you, and then you start tweaking onboarding and product based on their feedback, maybe you're going to dilute it for your must-have users." -- Sean Ellis

Focus on your "very disappointed" users first. Understand why they love you. Then expand from there.

### The Optimization Trap

> "You can't optimize your way to product market fit. I don't care at the early stages if something's optimized by 5% from an email. That doesn't fundamentally tell me if something's working or not." -- Claire Butler (Figma)

## Famous PMF Stories

### Superhuman -- The Engine in Action

Rahul Vohra used the Sean Ellis survey as an engine. He segmented users, identified that "very disappointed" users loved speed and keyboard shortcuts, then built for "somewhat disappointed" users who valued speed but had objections. Manually onboarded every user for years to ensure activation.

> "It becomes so powerful to have that early cohort of super fans." -- Rahul Vohra

### Gamma -- From Awards to Actual PMF

Grant Lee won every Product Hunt award in August 2022 but growth plateaued. Spent 3-4 months revamping onboarding with AI integration. Relaunched March 2023: went from hundreds of signups/day to thousands, then 5,000, then 10,000, then 20,000 -- all organic word of mouth.

> "It was just such a distinct difference between that feeling and coming out of the Product Hunt launch where we could have fooled ourselves into thinking we have product market fit." -- Grant Lee

### Lookout -- From 7% to Billion-Dollar Company

Sean Ellis's case study: Lookout started at 7% "very disappointed." They repositioned around antivirus and streamlined onboarding. Hit 40% in **two weeks**. Hit 60% six months later. Became a billion-dollar company.

### Nubank -- NPS of 94

Jag Duggal tapped into deep pain (NPS of -30 across Brazilian financial services). Operationalized Sean Ellis at 50% threshold for every product launch. In Mexico, NPS hit 94-95. 80-90% growth from word of mouth.

### Wise -- 8-10x Cheaper to Trigger Advocacy

Nilan Peiris discovered that being slightly cheaper than banks wasn't enough:

> "We only got the advocacy when we were eight to 10 times cheaper." -- Nilan Peiris

> "You have to give them an experience they didn't know was previously possible." -- Nilan Peiris

Wise NPS: 70%. Growth: 70% through word of mouth.

### Figma -- The Laptop Pull

Dylan Field demoed Figma to a potential customer who physically pulled the laptop from his hands. Figma spread bottoms-up -- the first Microsoft user came from the Xamarin team, not a top-down enterprise sale.

### Retool and Lattice -- The Inflection Point

David Hsu (Retool): "Every customer felt like the last we'd ever find" -- until suddenly it didn't. The boulder went over the hill.

Jack Altman (Lattice): "The biggest shift was in the ease of getting leads. I remember thinking, 'I don't even know where these leads are coming from.'"

## Losing PMF

PMF is not permanent. Uri Levine identifies two types of startup crisis: cash crisis and loss of PMF.

> "When product market fit disappears, you actually need to go back to square one." -- Uri Levine

Causes:
- **Regulation changes**: Uri's tax startup lost PMF overnight when the Israeli tax authority shut them down
- **Competition**: A better product makes yours obsolete
- **Market shifts**: "Nokia and Motorola -- their product market fit disappeared. People simply wanted something else."

Options when PMF is lost: pivot, shut down, or restart as a new company.

### PMF Must Be Maintained

> "Expectations of customers ever increase. And so you have product-market fit today, but there's going to be better products coming out." -- Casey Winters (quoted by Todd Jackson)

> "Just because you got to level four on your main product doesn't mean product-market fit is free on all these new products." -- Todd Jackson

## After PMF: The Growth Sequence

Sean Ellis defines the post-PMF sequence:

1. **Validate must-have** (40%+ on the survey)
2. **Optimize speed to value** (onboarding)
3. **Build engagement loop** (retention mechanics)
4. **Get existing users to bring new users** (virality/referrals)
5. **Optimize revenue model**
6. **THEN obsess on customer acquisition**

> "Customer acquisition is almost the last step." -- Sean Ellis

Four channels for growth: sales, SEO, virality/word of mouth, or paid growth.
